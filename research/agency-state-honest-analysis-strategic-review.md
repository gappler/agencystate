# Agency State: Honest Analysis & Strategic Review

You asked for hard truths, direct answers, and no softening. Based on a deep review of your site (agencystate.ai), the mid-2026 competitive landscape, and the current trajectory of AI coding tools, here is the unvarnished assessment of your business model.

## 1. Market Reality: Is there a real, paying market for this?

Yes, there is a massive, proven market for teaching non-developers to build software, but you are currently underpricing your offering and competing in a crowded space without clear differentiation.

The market for "vibe coding" and AI tool building for business professionals has exploded in 2026. A recent Retool survey found that 35% of companies have already replaced at least one SaaS tool with one they built themselves, and 78% expect to build more this year [1]. The default question in boardrooms has shifted from "what should we buy?" to "can we just build this?" [1].

However, the landscape is highly competitive. On Maven alone, there are currently 25 active courses in the "AI for Marketers" category [2]. 

**Who else is doing it well, and what are they charging?**
The most direct comparison to your offering is Natalie Lambert's (ex-Google) Maven course, "The Marketing Engineer: Vibe Coding for Non-Coders." She charges $899 for a 3-week cohort where students build four deployed apps using Claude Code, Supabase, and Vercel [3]. Other competitors include:
*   **General Assembly:** Their online "Vibe Coding" course costs $2,950 for an 8-week part-time program [4].
*   **University Executive Programs:** Institutions like Columbia and Cornell charge between $3,750 and $10,550 for short, intensive AI business courses [5].
*   **Independent Creators:** Instructors like Pietro Montaldo charge around $500-$800 for 2-4 week bootcamps, leveraging massive existing audiences (20k+ past students) [2].

**Where are their students coming from?**
They are not coming from cold traffic to a landing page. The most successful instructors acquire students through three primary channels:
1.  **Massive LinkedIn Audiences:** Instructors leverage existing followings of 10k-50k+ professionals, posting daily teardowns of AI tools.
2.  **Platform Organic Discovery:** Platforms like Maven act as marketplaces, driving internal traffic to highly-rated courses.
3.  **Niche AI Newsletters:** Sponsorships and features in newsletters like *The AI Break* or *AI Sprint* drive significant high-intent traffic [6].

**The Hard Truth:** At $500 for four sessions, you are priced at the absolute bottom of the professional cohort market. This signals low value to a marketing director who has a $2,000 professional development budget. You need to double your price to at least $899-$1,200 to be taken seriously as a professional intensive, and you must build an audience engine, as a standalone website will not convert cold traffic for a cohort course.

## 2. Site Honesty: The Marketing Director's Perspective

I read agencystate.ai through the lens of a marketing director with $500 to spend. The site has brilliant elements, but it currently contains fatal friction points that would make me close the tab.

**What would make them book the call?**
The interactive demos are your strongest asset. The Verdana Strategic Planning demo is genuinely impressive. It proves that the method works and produces tangible, high-value business outputs (market sizing, scenario modeling, OKRs) rather than generic chatbot text. The focus on "Context before tools" is also a strong differentiator; you are teaching strategic application, not just syntax.

**What would make them close the tab?**
The site is missing critical information required to make a purchasing decision, and the conversion path is broken.

*   **No Price Listed:** The $500 price tag is nowhere on the site. A marketing director will not book a "Call to confirm" without knowing if the program costs $500 or $5,000. This is the single biggest conversion killer.
*   **High-Friction CTA:** The only way to buy is to click "Call to confirm," which opens a `mailto:` link or requires a phone call. For a $500 product, buyers expect a frictionless, self-serve checkout (e.g., Stripe or a platform like Maven). Booking a call for a $500 product creates a mismatch in expected sales motion.
*   **Lack of Specific Outcomes:** The curriculum outlines four vague steps (Define context, Build tools, Produce content, Workshop problems). It does not tell the buyer *exactly* what they will walk away with. Will they have a deployed app? A local Python script? A set of prompts? 
*   **Missing Trust Signals:** There are no testimonials, no detailed instructor bio (beyond two sentences), no FAQ, and no refund policy. Furthermore, the demos are hosted on a separate domain (`gappler.github.io`), which slightly degrades trust.

**Specific Fixes Required:**
1.  Put the price ($899+) directly on the page.
2.  Implement a direct checkout link (Stripe) instead of a phone call.
3.  Add a detailed "What You Will Build" section outlining the exact deliverables.
4.  Move the demos to subdomains of `agencystate.ai` (e.g., `verdana.agencystate.ai`).

## 3. Faster Revenue: Done-For-You Services

Selling cohort seats requires a massive audience. Selling done-for-you (DFY) services requires only a few warm relationships. Using the exact same Claude Code skills you teach, you can generate $5K-$20K per project immediately. 

Here is the ranked list of DFY services by speed-to-revenue:

**1. Custom Internal Dashboards & Calculators ($5K - $10K)**
*   **The Pitch:** "Your team spends 10 hours a week manually pulling data into spreadsheets. I will build a custom, secure web app that automates this workflow for a one-time fee of $7,500."
*   **Why it's fast:** The pain point is acute, the ROI is easily measurable in saved labor hours, and you can build these in a few days using Claude Code, Supabase, and Vercel. This is exactly what Taylor Houck did at PointFive, replacing a manual content workflow with a custom app built entirely by Claude Code [1].

**2. AI Strategy & Scenario Modeling Systems ($10K - $15K)**
*   **The Pitch:** "I will build a bespoke strategic planning engine for your executive team, similar to the Verdana demo, allowing you to model market sizing and OKRs in real-time."
*   **Why it's fast:** You already have the templates and the code (the Verdana demo). You are selling the customization and the strategic implementation. This appeals directly to C-suite buyers who control larger budgets.

**3. Fractional Chief AI Officer (CAIO) / AI Implementation Retainer ($3K - $5K / month)**
*   **The Pitch:** "I will audit your agency's workflows, identify bottlenecks, and build 2-3 custom AI tools per month to automate your operations."
*   **Why it's fast:** Agencies are desperate to integrate AI but lack the technical talent. You become their outsourced AI engineering department. Standard rates for fractional AI leadership range from $100-$500 per hour [7].

These DFY projects will generate immediate cash flow and provide the exact case studies you need to sell the cohort later. "I charge $10,000 to build this for clients, or you can pay $1,000 to learn how to build it yourself in my cohort."

## 4. Shelf Life: Are you building on sand?

Anthropic ships fast. In Q1 2026 alone, they released a major update every two weeks [8]. On April 14, 2026, they released a redesigned Claude Code desktop app with a GUI "Mission Control" sidebar, moving away from a pure terminal experience [9]. 

**Does your positioning survive?**
If your positioning is "I will teach you the syntax of Claude Code," your shelf life is less than six months. The tools are rapidly converging into autonomous, no-code, drag-and-drop interfaces. Cursor, Lovable, and Claude Code are all racing to eliminate the terminal entirely [10].

However, your current positioning—**"Context before tools"**—is highly resilient. 

The New Stack recently noted that while AI coding tools are making software development accessible to non-developers, the real value lies in building "personal software"—highly customized tools that solve specific, niche business problems [1]. AI can write the code, but it cannot define the business context, understand the strategic nuances of a DTC wellness brand, or map the political landscape of a 40-person agency.

**The Pivot Strategy:**
You are not teaching people how to code; you are teaching them **Systems Thinking for the AI Era**. 

If Claude Code becomes a one-click voice interface tomorrow, your students still need to know *what* to ask it to build. They still need to understand data architecture, workflow mapping, and how to connect a strategic goal to a technical output. 

To ensure longevity, double down on the "Agency State Method" (defining context, mapping workflows, structuring data) rather than the specific tool (Claude Code). Position Claude Code as the current vehicle, but sell the methodology. If the tool changes, the method remains the same.
