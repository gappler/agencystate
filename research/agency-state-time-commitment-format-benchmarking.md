# Agency State: Time Commitment & Format Benchmarking

You asked a critical question: how does your proposed format (4 sessions, 2 hours each, over 4 weeks, plus homework) compare to what is actually selling in the market right now? 

The short answer is that your format is **perfectly aligned with the "Classic" cohort model** that platforms like Maven recommend for the $500–$1,000 price point. However, the market is currently rewarding *intensity and speed* over drawn-out schedules.

Here is the unvarnished breakdown of the actual time commitments required by your direct competitors, followed by an analysis of what converts best.

## How Your Format Compares

Your proposed format (8 hours of live instruction over 4 weeks) sits right in the middle of the pack in terms of duration, but is actually **heavier on mandatory live instruction** than almost all of your direct competitors under $1,000.

### 1. The Market is Rewarding "Speed to Outcome"
Notice the trend in the data above: Pietro Montaldo ($399), Vijay Patel ($215), and Yuying Chen-Wynn ($800) are all compressing their courses into 2 weeks, 5 days, or a single weekend. They are selling *speed*. 

Yuying Chen-Wynn is charging $800 for a single weekend bootcamp with only 4-6 hours of live instruction. She is explicitly targeting "busy professionals who keep hearing about AI app tools but don’t have time to chase hype." [1]

### 2. The "Classic" Format vs. The "Intensive" Format
Maven categorizes cohort courses into three main formats: Classic, Weekend Seminar, and Hybrid. [2]

Your 4-week, 4-session format is the textbook definition of a "Classic" cohort course. Maven explicitly recommends this format for first-time instructors, stating: "For your first cohort, four 90-minute sessions over two weeks is a great place to start." [3]

However, you are spreading those 4 sessions over 4 weeks. For a highly technical, momentum-driven topic like Claude Code, a 4-week spread can lead to drop-off. If a student gets stuck on an environment setup issue in Week 1, they have to wait a full week for the next live session to get unblocked.

### 3. The "Optional" Office Hours Strategy
Look closely at Natalie Lambert's $899 course. She advertises "13 live sessions," which sounds massive. But when you read the syllabus, there are only 3 mandatory core instructional sessions (1.5 hours each). The rest are optional 30-minute "Group Unblock Me" sessions and bookable 1:1 time. [4]

This is a brilliant pricing strategy. It creates a high perceived value (13 sessions!) while keeping the mandatory time commitment low for busy professionals (4.5 hours total).

## Strategic Recommendations for Agency State

Based on the market data, your $500 price point is highly competitive, but your format could be optimized for better conversion and completion rates.

**1. Compress the Timeline**
Consider compressing your 4 sessions into 2 weeks (e.g., Tuesdays and Thursdays). Momentum is critical when learning to code. A 2-week intensive feels more actionable and less daunting to a busy marketing director than a month-long commitment.

**2. Shorten Core Sessions, Add "Unblock" Sessions**
Two hours of live technical instruction via Zoom is exhausting. Consider shifting to 60-90 minute core instructional sessions, immediately followed by optional 30-minute "Unblock Me" office hours. This matches the format used by the most successful instructors in this space.

**3. Price to Value, Not to Hours**
Do not fall into the trap of thinking "more hours = higher price." Yuying Chen-Wynn is charging $800 for a single weekend. You are selling a transformation (the ability to build custom tools), not an hourly tutoring rate. If you can deliver that transformation in 6 hours instead of 8, the course is actually *more* valuable to a busy professional.

**4. Frame the Homework as "Building Your Portfolio"**
Marketers don't want "homework." They want assets. Frame the between-session work exactly as Natalie Lambert does: "Stop practicing and start shipping. Each week, you will build and deploy a custom tool for your actual job." [4]

Your format is solid, but tweaking the pacing and framing will make it significantly easier to sell at $500—or even $750.
