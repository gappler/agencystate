# Broadening the Frame: Marketing Teams → Business Teams
## Research Brief for Gregory — Round 3

**Core question:** Does broadening from "teaching marketing teams to build with Claude Code" to "teaching business teams to build custom tools with Claude Code" meaningfully expand the opportunity without diluting the positioning?

**Answer:** Yes — but with a specific architecture. The Retool 2026 Build vs. Buy Report just handed you the macro thesis on a silver platter: 35% of enterprises have already replaced SaaS tools with custom builds, and 78% expect to build more in 2026. The build-vs-buy shift is happening across every business function, not just marketing. Broadening to "business teams" roughly 5x the addressable market. But the competitive risk is real: going broad too early means competing with generic vibe coding courses, corporate AI training programs, and Anthropic's own free courses. The right move is a hub-and-spoke architecture — lead with marketing as the proof domain (where you have credibility, examples, and zero competition), then expand spoke by spoke into operations, sales ops, and finance as the curriculum and case studies mature.

---

## 1. The build vs. buy data changes everything

Retool's February 2026 report, based on a survey of 817 builders across functions, is the single most important data point for this decision. The headline findings:

**35% of teams have already replaced at least one SaaS tool with a custom build.** This isn't aspirational — it's done. And 78% expect to build more custom internal tools in 2026. The build-vs-buy equation has tipped, driven by AI-assisted development making custom tools achievable in days rather than months.

**Every SaaS category is under replacement pressure.** Workflow automations (35%) and internal admin tools (33%) lead the list. CRMs and form builders (25%), BI tools (29%), project management (23%), and customer support tools (21%) are all facing custom-build competition from within the organizations that pay for them.

**The builders span every function.** Retool's survey respondents include engineering (36%), operations (12%), product management (10%), data (7%), marketing and sales ops (6%), IT (6%), business analysis (4%), and finance (4%). The key insight: 64% of the builders are not software engineers. Operations and product management together represent 22% — a larger segment than marketing/sales ops.

**60% have built software outside IT oversight in the past year.** 25% do so frequently. This is shadow-building at scale, driven by the same dynamics as shadow IT but now involving actual tool creation, not just unauthorized SaaS purchases.

The SaaS waste data amplifies this. According to multiple 2025–2026 analyses: enterprises waste approximately $18–21 million annually on unused or underutilized licenses. 51% of SaaS licenses go unused — the highest rate ever recorded. 53% of SaaS applications go underutilized. The average enterprise now manages 275–291 SaaS applications at $4,200–$9,643 per employee annually. 56% of SaaS purchases happen outside IT oversight (shadow IT).

**What this means for Gregory:** The "build your own" value proposition isn't niche — it's mainstream. The pain is universal across functions. The behavior is already happening. What's missing is structured capability-building that teaches non-developers to build responsibly and well, rather than the current reality of unguided shadow-building with no governance, no security awareness, and no quality standards.

## 2. How the addressable market changes when you broaden

**Marketing → Business roughly 5x the opportunity.**

In the marketing-only frame, the addressable persona was roughly 5–10% of marketing professionals actively using AI — call it 200,000–500,000 people in the US alone. The realistic training buyers (teams with budget for upskilling) were a small fraction of that.

Broadening to business professionals multiplies by function count. Operations, sales ops, finance, HR, product management, customer success, and general business analysis all show the same pattern: tech-forward professionals using AI daily who want to build but lack structured capability. The Retool data shows operations alone represents 12% of active builders — twice the share of marketing/sales ops.

**Which functions show the strongest builder demand?**

Based on the Retool survey, SaaS waste data, and citizen developer research:

**Operations is the largest non-engineering builder population.** 12% of Retool's builder base. Operations teams sit at the intersection of multiple data sources and workflows — they're the people most frustrated by tools that are "70% right." Their builds tend to be workflow automations, admin dashboards, and internal process tools — exactly what's easiest to build with Claude Code.

**Sales operations has intense custom-tool needs.** CRMs are under 25% replacement pressure per Retool. Every sales team has a different process, different data sources, different reporting needs. The gap between what Salesforce provides out of the box and what any specific sales team actually needs is where custom tools live. Sales ops professionals tend to be analytically minded and comfortable with data — good builder candidates.

**Finance has the budget authority and the pain.** Finance teams manage the most expensive SaaS stacks and are the first to feel overspend. 67% of CFOs now rank software cost management as a top-three priority. Finance professionals who can build their own reporting tools, forecasting dashboards, and reconciliation automations create massive ROI. However, compliance and auditability requirements make this a more sensitive domain — builds need governance.

**Product management is already building.** 10% of Retool's builder base. PMs are the natural bridge between business need and technical implementation, and the Maven ecosystem already has multiple courses targeting PMs who want to prototype (Marily Nika's AI PM Bootcamp, Harold Dijkstra's Prototyping with AI Bootcamp). This means more competition in the PM-specific space.

**HR has the lowest technical comfort and highest compliance sensitivity.** While HR teams face real build-vs-buy pain (especially in onboarding workflows, benefits administration, and compliance tracking), HR professionals are generally the least technically adventurous non-developer population. This function is likely a later expansion, not an early one.

**The Gartner citizen developer data adds structural support.** Citizen developers now outnumber professional developers 4:1 at large enterprises. 80% of low-code users in 2026 are professionals outside IT departments. 100–120 million people globally are regularly building business applications. 41% of non-IT workers customize or create data and application solutions. These aren't aspirational projections — they're current measurements.

## 3. The "build vs. buy" pain across business functions

**The marketing case is the clearest and most emotionally resonant.** Marketing teams overpay for analytics ($200–$500/month per tool), email automation ($300–$1,500/month), SEO tools ($100–$500/month), attribution tools ($500–$2,000/month), and competitive intelligence ($300–$1,000/month). A five-person marketing team at a DTC brand can easily spend $3,000–$8,000/month on tools that cover 70% of their needs. The custom-build case: replace $60K–$96K/year in SaaS spend with $5K–$10K in Claude Code training and $500–$2,000/year in API costs.

**Operations has the widest gap between generic tools and specific needs.** Every operations team has unique workflows shaped by their specific business processes. Off-the-shelf project management, ticketing, and workflow tools force teams to adapt their processes to the tool's assumptions. The Retool data confirms this: workflow automations and internal admin tools face the highest replacement pressure (35% and 33% respectively). Operations is where the "this tool is 70% right" frustration is strongest.

**Finance has the highest per-tool cost and the clearest ROI math.** Finance SaaS stacks at growth-stage companies run $25K–$100K+ annually for billing (Maxio at $25K–$40K/year), forecasting (Mosaic at $30K+/year), and revenue recognition tools. Custom-built financial reporting dashboards, reconciliation tools, and forecast models using Claude Code could replace or supplement these at a fraction of the cost — with the crucial advantage of being perfectly tailored to the company's specific metrics and data sources. However, the audit trail and compliance requirements in finance make this the highest-stakes domain for custom builds.

**Sales ops faces the "Salesforce gap" — the difference between CRM as configured and CRM as needed.** Companies spend $75–$300/user/month on Salesforce plus an ecosystem of add-on tools for pipeline visibility, forecasting, and reporting. Custom internal tools that sit alongside the CRM — pulling data, transforming it, and presenting it in ways the CRM can't — are among the most common builds by non-engineer builders.

**Customer support has a simpler build-vs-buy equation.** Support tools (Zendesk, Intercom, Freshdesk) are relatively mature and well-configured for most use cases. The custom-build opportunity is more narrow: internal knowledge bases, ticket routing logic, and reporting dashboards. Under 21% replacement pressure per Retool — the lowest of the major categories.

## 4. Does broadening weaken or strengthen the competitive position?

**Broadening introduces more adjacent competition but doesn't eliminate the whitespace.**

In the marketing-only frame, the finding from Round 2 was definitive: zero competitors at "Claude Code training for marketing teams." Broadening to "business teams" introduces overlap with:

**Generic vibe coding education (crowded, mostly cheap/free).** 30+ courses on Coursera, dozens on Udemy, Maven cohorts — but all teach "how to vibe code" generically, not "how to build custom internal tools for your specific business function." The distinction matters: a $20 Udemy course teaches you to make an app. Gregory would teach you to replace $2,000/month in SaaS spend with a tool that does exactly what your team needs. Outcome-specific training vs. tool-generic training.

**Corporate AI training providers (expensive, AI-usage focused).** Edstellar, GAIN Academy, Google's Grow with Google program, DataCamp for Business — all teach AI literacy and usage, not AI building. The EY 2025 Work Reimagined Survey found 88% of employees use AI at work, but only 5% in transformative ways. These programs address the 88% (using AI) not the 5% (transforming work with AI). Building custom tools is the transformation layer that none of them teach.

**The Maven/cohort ecosystem (closest competitors, but different positioning).** Harold Dijkstra's Prototyping with AI Bootcamp ($799, 1,500+ students) targets PMs and entrepreneurs wanting to build products — not business professionals wanting to build internal tools. Marily Nika's AI PM Bootcamp ($1,500+) targets product managers specifically. Neither teaches Claude Code, neither focuses on internal-tool-building for business teams, neither addresses the build-vs-buy ROI case.

**Anthropic's own training (free, developer-focused).** Anthropic Academy's 13 free courses plus the Claude Certified Architect credential serve the developer and enterprise architect audience. They have nothing targeting business professionals building internal tools. The Cowork course comes closest but is tool-instruction, not business-outcome-instruction.

**Net competitive assessment:** Broadening introduces adjacency but not direct competition. The specific niche of "teaching business teams to build custom internal tools with Claude Code, with clear build-vs-buy ROI" remains unoccupied regardless of whether you say "marketing teams" or "business teams." The key differentiator isn't the audience label — it's the outcome framing: "replace expensive SaaS that's 70% right with custom tools that are 100% yours."

## 5. The right positioning architecture

**Lead broad, prove with marketing, expand by function.**

The research clearly supports a hub-and-spoke model:

**The hub: "We teach business teams to build their own tools with Claude Code."** This is the brand position, the website headline, the LinkedIn tagline. It captures the full addressable market and aligns with the macro trend (Retool's build-vs-buy shift, citizen developer movement, vibe coding explosion). It doesn't limit future expansion and it matches how buyers think — a COO or VP of Operations searching for "teach my team to build tools with AI" is a real buyer. A "marketer" searching for the same thing is also captured by this positioning.

**The first spoke: Marketing.** This is where Gregory leads with proof. The curriculum starts here because: you have deep credibility (decade at AIA, agency background, brand-side roles); the marketing build-vs-buy case is the most emotionally resonant and easiest to demonstrate; you have zero competition in this specific space; marketing tools have the shortest time-to-value (build a competitive dashboard in an hour vs. build a financial reconciliation tool that needs audit compliance). First cohorts are "Build Your Own MarTech" — a 4-week program where marketing teams leave with working tools.

**Second spoke: Operations/Sales Ops.** These functions represent the largest non-engineer builder population (22% combined in Retool's data) and have the strongest build-vs-buy pain (workflow automations at 35% replacement pressure). The curriculum adapts: same Claude Code fundamentals, different build projects. "Build Your Own Ops Tools" or "Build Your Own Sales Intelligence Stack."

**Third spoke: Finance.** Highest budget authority, strongest ROI math, but highest compliance sensitivity. This spoke requires more careful curriculum design around governance, audit trails, and security. It's the premium offering — finance teams will pay more because the stakes are higher.

**Precedents for this architecture:** Several training businesses have successfully broadened from one function to many. CXL started as a conversion rate optimization training platform for marketers, then expanded to growth marketing, then to product management, then to data analytics — maintaining credibility by proving expertise in each domain before expanding. Marketing AI Institute started with marketing-specific AI education and is now expanding to general enterprise AI readiness through MAICON and corporate workshops. The pattern: establish authority in one domain, build curriculum infrastructure, then replicate the framework across domains with function-specific content.

**Risks of going too broad too early:**

First risk: you lose the specificity that makes the marketing positioning compelling. "We teach business teams to build" is weaker than "we teach marketing teams to build their own competitive intelligence, attribution, and reporting tools" because the second version lets the buyer immediately see the outcome. Broad positioning without proof points in each function creates the "we do everything for everyone" problem.

Second risk: curriculum quality suffers. Building a genuinely excellent 4-week program for marketing teams requires deep understanding of marketing workflows, tools, pain points, and success metrics. Trying to simultaneously build curricula for marketing, operations, sales ops, and finance dilutes the effort.

Third risk: you compete with yourself for attention. A marketing team looking for training isn't browsing "general business AI building courses" — they're searching for marketing-specific solutions. A broad positioning might rank lower in search and generate less trust than a specific one.

**Mitigation:** The website and brand say "business teams." The first three cohorts and all launch content say "marketing teams." The progression is natural — early marketing cohort participants will say "can you do this for our ops team too?" That's the organic expansion signal.

## 6. Best target verticals and company sizes

**The previous ranking holds but the sweet spot narrows.**

For capability-building training (vs. consulting), the ideal buyer profile shifts slightly:

**Company size sweet spot: 25–200 employees.** This is where "your team builds its own tools" resonates most powerfully. Below 25 employees, you're typically training a founder or solo operator — valuable but not a team training sale. Above 200 employees, you start hitting enterprise procurement processes, IT governance requirements, and the expectation that engineering builds internal tools. The 25–200 range is where: teams wear multiple hats and feel the SaaS-doesn't-fit pain most acutely; there's no dedicated internal tools team; budget exists for training ($5K–$15K) but not for custom development ($50K–$200K); the decision-maker (VP, COO, founder) can say yes without a procurement cycle.

**E-commerce/DTC remains #1.** Fast decision cycles, high SaaS spend relative to revenue, technically adventurous teams, and clear build-vs-buy ROI. A 50-person DTC brand spending $15K/month on marketing and ops SaaS is the perfect buyer for a "build your own" training program.

**Digital agencies move up to #1b.** For the broader "business teams" positioning, agencies are extraordinarily compelling. They have multiple functions (marketing, sales, ops, client delivery) all suffering from SaaS overload. They have a multiplier effect: an agency that can build custom tools for clients has a fundamentally different capability than one using off-the-shelf tools. And they tend to have the most technically curious non-developer staff, because their business is technology-adjacent.

**SaaS companies (Series A–B) are a strong #2.** Startup teams are already the most active builders — 25% of YC W25 had 95%+ AI-generated codebases. Their operations, sales, and marketing teams are small, resource-constrained, and hungry for capability that doesn't require hiring engineers. The culture already values building.

**Professional services firms remain #3** with the caveat that the entry point is marketing (where they're investing for the first time) and expansion is into operations and client delivery.

**Associations drop further.** For capability-building training specifically, association budgets ($2K–$5K per team) are below the price point needed to sustain quality live cohort programs. Associations are better served with lower-priced async content or community offerings — the spoke that comes last, not first.

**Industry-specific fit matters more than function-specific fit.** A 75-person e-commerce company's marketing team, ops team, and finance team all benefit from custom-tool-building capability. Selling "build training for your whole company" is a higher-value proposition than selling function by function. This argues for the broad positioning — as long as you can prove value in at least one function for each company profile.

**Position broad. Prove narrow. Expand by demand.**

**Brand:** Gregory teaches business teams to build their own tools with Claude Code. The macro story: the build-vs-buy shift is here — 35% of teams have already replaced SaaS with custom builds, 78% plan to build more, and your team can too.

**Second product (months 3–6):** "Build Your Own Ops Stack" — same format, adapted for operations teams. Workflow automations, internal dashboards, admin tools. This is where 35% of Retool's replacement pressure sits.

Marketing, ops, and sales ops in one engagement at $8K–$15K. This is the real revenue play: instead of selling one cohort seat at $2K, you sell a company-wide capability at 5–8x.

Business-teams positioning, with the hub-and-spoke product architecture, raises the ceiling to $500K–$800K in year one (same cohort model, broader buyer pool) and $800K–$1.2M by year two (company-level engagements + skills library subscriptions + corporate workshops).
