# The Builder Marketer: Research Brief for Gregory

**Research question:** Is "enabling builder marketers" — training tech-savvy marketing professionals to build custom tools with Claude Code and similar AI development environments — a defensible niche with real demand, or an interesting idea without a proven market?

**Bottom line:** The demand is real, the timing is right, and the specific niche of "teaching marketing teams to build with Claude Code" is almost entirely unclaimed. But the opportunity is narrower and more nuanced than it first appears. The market isn't "all marketers learning to code" — it's a specific segment of technically curious professionals ready to cross from prompting to building, and the competitive landscape is forming fast.

---

## 1. Who is currently teaching non-developers to build with AI coding tools?

The vibe coding education market exploded in 2025–2026 and is now a distinct category with real revenue, though almost none of it targets marketers specifically.

**The landscape breaks into five tiers:**

**Tier 1: Premium live cohorts on Maven.** The most directly relevant model. Harold Dijkstra and Ciara Wearen run the Prototyping with AI Bootcamp — a 2.5-week live cohort at **$799/person** that has enrolled **1,500+ students** across 14 cohorts. Target audience: product managers, marketers, and designers with zero coding skills. Tools covered include Lovable, Bolt.new, Replit, v0, and Supabase. Students from Microsoft, Amazon, Shopify, Meta, and Canva. This is the closest existing model to what Gregory would build, but it's tool-agnostic (not Claude-specific) and product-manager-focused (not marketing-workflow-focused). Maven also hosts AI Prototyping for Product Managers, various No-Code x AI Bootcamps, and the AI Engineering Bootcamp by AI Maker Space ($1,500+, targeting actual engineers). Maven courses average around $500–$800 for multi-week cohorts.

**Tier 2: Free community-driven courses.** CCforEveryone.com (created by Carl Vellotti) is the most notable — a free, interactive Claude Code course specifically designed for non-engineers. The course teaches Claude Code inside Claude Code itself, covering file operations, parallel agents, custom sub-agents, project memory via CLAUDE.md, and a full vibe coding module. It includes community contributions from Greg Isenberg interviews and advanced workflow guides. This is a passion project, not a business — no pricing, no cohort model, no revenue. It has a GitHub repository and growing community but no formal monetization. Its existence proves demand; its lack of business model proves the commercial gap.

**Tier 3: Platform courses (Coursera, Udemy, LinkedIn Learning).** Anthropic itself published "Claude Code in Action" on Coursera — a free, self-paced course covering CLI setup, development workflows, and practical applications. Coursera now lists 30+ vibe coding courses including "The Non-Coder's Guide to Vibe Coding" (SkillsBooster Academy, free with certificate option). Udemy has dozens of vibe coding courses ($20–$100 range), mostly targeting developers wanting to ship faster, not non-developers wanting to start building. LinkedIn Learning has a vibe coding introduction course. None of these target marketers specifically. They're general-purpose.

**Tier 4: YouTube and content creators.** A rapidly growing content layer around Claude Code for marketers is emerging. Stormy AI's blog has published at least six detailed guides on Claude Code for marketing in early 2026 alone, covering everything from installation for non-technical users to building SEO command centers and PPC automation. Geeky Gadgets runs a series on building AI marketing teams with Claude Skills. Strategic Nerds published a detailed guide on automating product marketing with Claude. AdventurePPC wrote a comprehensive piece explicitly titled "What Is Claude Code? The AI Coding Assistant Every Marketer Needs to Know in 2026." These are content plays, not training businesses — but they signal genuine demand for this exact knowledge.

**Tier 5: The AI automation agency ecosystem.** Liam Ottley's AAA Accelerator (3,800+ students, 8,400+ community members) teaches building AI automation workflows with n8n, Make.com, and Claude Code — but targets aspiring agency owners, not in-house marketers. Similarly, Zubair Trabzada published an open-source "AI Marketing Suite for Claude Code" on GitHub with 15 marketing skills and parallel subagents, pitched at "entrepreneurs, agency builders, and solopreneurs who want to sell marketing services powered by AI." The agency training space is getting crowded; the in-house marketer training space is wide open.

**Pricing benchmarks across the landscape:**
- Maven live cohorts: $499–$999 for 2–4 week programs
- Corporate workshops: $3,000–$10,000/day (solo consultant); $75,000+ (Marketing AI Institute)
- Udemy self-paced: $20–$100
- Coursera specializations: Free to $49/month subscription
- Anthropic Academy: Free (13 courses on Skilljar)

## 2. How real is the "builder marketer" as a persona?

The evidence is strong that non-developers are building with AI tools at scale. The evidence for marketers specifically doing this is emerging but not yet quantified separately.

**The macro data is unambiguous.** According to a comprehensive state-of-vibe-coding report compiled from Gartner, McKinsey, Stack Overflow, and GitHub data (updated March 2026): **63% of vibe coding users are non-developers.** They're generating UIs (44%), full-stack apps (20%), and personal software (11%). The vibe coding tools market reached **$4.7 billion in 2025** and is projected at $12.3 billion by 2027 (38% CAGR). Collins Dictionary named "vibe coding" Word of the Year for 2025. In Y Combinator's Winter 2025 batch, 25% of startups had codebases 95%+ AI-generated.

**The citizen developer movement provides structural context.** Gartner estimates that citizen developers now outnumber professional developers roughly 4:1 at large enterprises. Between 100 and 120 million people globally are regularly building business applications in no-code and low-code platforms. 80% of technology products and services will be built by people outside traditional IT roles by 2026, per Gartner. 60% of custom apps are now built outside IT departments, with 30% built by employees with limited or no technical skills. Organizations that empower citizen developers score 33% higher on innovation measures (McKinsey).

**The "marketer-developer" convergence is documented.** Multiple sources from early 2026 describe the emergence of what Stormy AI calls the "marketing engineer" — a marketer who can operate CLI-based tools, write prompts that generate functional code, and deploy custom automation. The Salesforce State of Marketing 10th Edition (early 2026) reports that 64% of high-growth marketing teams now utilize CLI-based agents for tasks like technical SEO. Anthropic's own data reportedly shows API revenue from non-developer roles surging 410% (though this claim comes from a third-party blog and should be verified). One product marketing manager at Strategic Nerds documented building an entire system of Claude-powered marketing tools — competitive intelligence, content repurposing, positioning frameworks — that replaced hours of manual work.

**But the "builder marketer" segment is still a subset of a subset.** The 78% of workers who bring their own AI tools to work are mostly prompting, not building. The 63% of vibe coders who are non-developers include product managers, designers, entrepreneurs, and many others — not just marketers. The realistic addressable population for "marketers who want to build custom tools" is probably 5–10% of the marketing professionals actively using AI, which translates to tens of thousands of people, not millions. That's big enough for a solo practice, but it's a niche, not a mass market.

**The security and quality concerns are real constraints.** AI-generated code has 1.7x more major issues and 2.74x higher security vulnerability rates than human-written code (CodeRabbit analysis of 470 pull requests). 45% of AI-generated code fails security tests (Veracode). 10.3% of Lovable-generated apps had critical security flaws. These risks are manageable with proper review practices, but they mean that "marketer builds production app with no oversight" is genuinely dangerous — and that training must include quality assurance and security awareness, not just building speed. This actually strengthens the case for expert training rather than self-guided learning.

## 3. Which target markets value "builder marketer" training most?

The research from the first analysis established vertical readiness for AI consulting broadly. Here's how that maps specifically to builder-marketer training:

**E-commerce/DTC brands ($3M–$50M revenue) are the highest-value target.** Their marketing teams are typically 1–5 people wearing multiple hats. They face acute pain from expensive SaaS tool stacks — paying $500–$2,000/month each for analytics, email, attribution, competitive intelligence, and reporting tools. A marketer who can build custom dashboards, automated reporting pipelines, and API integrations with Claude Code can replace $5,000–$15,000/month in software subscriptions. The ROI case writes itself. These teams tend to be technically adventurous (they live in Shopify, manage pixels, run A/B tests) and make decisions fast.

**Digital agencies and marketing consultancies are a natural second market.** They have the strongest business case: a team that can build custom client tools creates proprietary competitive advantage. Agencies already sell "custom" solutions that are often just configured SaaS tools — the ability to actually build purpose-built tools would be transformative. Agency teams tend to have higher technical baseline skills (working with HTML, CSS, analytics code, API integrations) and shorter sales cycles for training investments. They also have a multiplier effect: train one agency team and they build tools for dozens of clients.

**Startups (seed through Series B) are a high-intent but price-sensitive market.** These companies have technical founders who understand the value of building but marketing teams that can't. A marketing hire who can build their own tools is dramatically more valuable. However, budget constraints and high churn make this a better market for lower-priced products (courses, cohorts) than high-ticket consulting.

**Professional services firms have the budget but slower adoption.** Law firms and accounting firms are investing heavily in marketing for the first time, but their marketing teams are generally less technical than DTC or agency marketers. This market needs more hand-holding and is better served through the workshop model than self-directed building.

**Associations remain the lowest-budget but most uncontested market.** No one is teaching association marketing teams to build with AI tools. The typical association marketing team (2–4 people, constrained budget, high need for member engagement automation) would benefit enormously from builder capability. But the budget for training is $2K–$5K, not $10K–$25K. This remains a positioning play and community-building opportunity rather than a primary revenue driver.

## 4. Anthropic's ecosystem: what's happening and what it means for Gregory

Anthropic made a massive strategic move in March 2026 that changes the landscape for anyone building a Claude-focused practice.

**The Claude Partner Network launched March 12, 2026 with $100 million in backing.** This is Anthropic's AWS-style ecosystem play. Key details: membership is free for any organization bringing Claude to market. Partners get access to the Partner Portal, Anthropic Academy training materials, sales playbooks, co-marketing funds, and listing in a Services Partner Directory for enterprise buyers. Anthropic is scaling its partner-facing team fivefold, providing dedicated Applied AI engineers, technical architects, and localized go-to-market support.

**The first Claude Certified Architect (CCA) credential launched simultaneously.** It's a proctored, 60-question architecture-level exam — not a prompting quiz. 45% of the exam covers agentic architecture and Claude Code configuration. Free for the first 5,000 partner employees, then $99. No retakes currently. More certifications for sellers, developers, and advanced architects are planned for later 2026. This is explicitly positioned as an enterprise credential — closer to an AWS Solutions Architect certification than an AI literacy badge.

**Anthropic Academy offers 13 free courses on Skilljar.** These include Claude 101, Building Applications with the Claude API, Introduction to Model Context Protocol, Claude Code in Action, Claude Code Skills, Claude Code Sub-agents, and a Cowork course. All are self-paced and free. Anthropic also partnered with CodePath (focused on students) and the American Federation of Teachers (1.8 million members) for broader AI education. Their Coursera course on Claude Code is also free.

**The enterprise focus creates both opportunity and limitation for Gregory.** The Partner Network is designed for consulting firms, professional services organizations, and system integrators — not solo trainers teaching marketers. But joining is free, and the certification + Academy materials provide legitimate, vendor-endorsed credibility. The CCA certification is overkill for a marketing-focused practice, but the forthcoming "developer" certification tier (later 2026) could be directly relevant.

**The critical gap in Anthropic's ecosystem:** All of their training is either (a) enterprise/developer-focused or (b) general-purpose AI literacy. They have nothing that bridges the specific gap between "marketer who uses Claude for prompting" and "marketer who builds custom tools with Claude Code." The Cowork course comes closest — teaching non-developers to delegate file operations to Claude — but it's tool-agnostic about use cases. No one in Anthropic's training ecosystem is teaching marketing-specific applications of Claude Code's building capabilities.

**Claude Code pricing and accessibility:** Claude Code is included in Claude Pro ($20/month) and Team ($30/user/month) plans, with usage-based pricing through the API. Average daily cost is reportedly about $6, with 90% of users spending less than $12/day. The Team Premium tier ($150/user/month) offers centralized billing and MCP server sharing — relevant for agencies. The tools require Node.js installation and basic terminal comfort, which is a real barrier for non-technical marketers. Cursor ($20/month) is an alternative that provides a more visual IDE experience. Lovable ($20–$50/month) and Bolt.new are browser-based options that eliminate terminal anxiety entirely.

## 5. Competitive whitespace analysis

**The central finding: nobody is specifically positioning as "I teach marketing teams to build with Claude Code."** Not one person or company occupies this exact niche.

**Here's what exists in adjacent spaces:**

**Vibe coding education (crowded, generic, mostly free).** 30+ courses on Coursera, dozens on Udemy, multiple Maven cohorts, YouTube tutorials. The Prototyping with AI Bootcamp ($799, 1,500+ students) is the closest format match but doesn't target marketers and doesn't teach Claude Code specifically. Competition is fierce at the low end (free tutorials, $20 Udemy courses) but thin at the premium end (expert-led cohorts for specific professional audiences).

**AI marketing education (established, but focused on using AI, not building with it).** Paul Roetzer's Marketing AI Institute dominates the broad space (100K+ subscribers, $75K+ corporate workshops, MAICON conference). CXL offers AI marketing courses ($289–$2,698/year). The ANA runs AI marketing bootcamps ($1,295–$1,895). But all of these teach marketers to use AI tools — not to build custom tools with AI. The distinction is fundamental: "learn to use Claude for content creation" versus "learn to build a custom competitive intelligence dashboard with Claude Code." No one in this category has crossed that line.

**Claude Code for marketing (content exists, training doesn't).** The Stormy AI blog has published at least six comprehensive guides on Claude Code for marketers. AdventurePPC wrote a detailed guide explicitly calling Claude Code "the AI coding assistant every marketer needs to know." Strategic Nerds documented a full marketing automation system built with Claude Skills and MCP. All of these are content — articles, blog posts, tutorials. None are structured training programs. None charge money. None offer cohort-based learning, implementation support, or community. The content proves the demand; the absence of training proves the gap.

**AI automation agency training (crowded, but different audience).** Liam Ottley's AAA Accelerator teaches building AI automation workflows, but targets people who want to start agencies — not in-house marketers building for their own teams. The distinction matters: agency training is about selling services; builder-marketer training is about building internal capability. Different buyer, different use case, different pricing.

**Corporate AI training providers (expensive, generic).** Harvard DCE ($3,200 for two-day programs), consulting firms (Accenture training 30,000 on Claude, Cognizant enabling 350,000), and enterprise training platforms are all investing in AI upskilling. But they're focused on general AI fluency, not on specific building skills for marketing professionals. Their pricing ($5K–$75K+ per engagement) is appropriate for enterprise but inaccessible for small marketing teams.

**The open-source / free community layer.** CCforEveryone.com teaches Claude Code to non-engineers for free. GitHub repositories like the AI Marketing Suite for Claude Code provide open-source marketing skills. These serve the self-directed learner but don't provide structured learning paths, accountability, live instruction, or community. They're reference materials, not training programs.

**Gaps that represent specific opportunities for Gregory:**

First: no one teaches Claude Code specifically to marketing teams in a structured, paid format. The intersection of "Claude Code" + "marketing professionals" + "paid training" has zero occupants.

Second: no one bridges the gap between "marketer using Claude for prompting" and "marketer building custom tools with Claude Code." Every existing program is either (a) general AI prompting for marketers, or (b) general vibe coding for anyone. The specific journey of a marketer going from power user to builder is unserved.

Third: no one offers a Claude-specific training practice that leverages the Claude Partner Network ecosystem. With Anthropic investing $100 million in partner enablement, a practice built around Claude Code training for specific professional audiences could access co-marketing funds, Partner Portal resources, and directory listing — a structural advantage that tool-agnostic trainers can't access.

Fourth: the mid-market pricing tier between free tutorials and $75K corporate workshops is wide open for marketing-specific AI building programs. A $1,500–$2,500 live cohort targeting marketing teams of 3–10 people at companies doing $3M–$100M in revenue has no direct competitor.

Fifth: no one is building productized Claude Code assets (skills, templates, workflows) specifically for marketing teams. The GitHub AI Marketing Suite exists as an open-source project, but a curated, maintained, supported library of marketing-specific Claude Code skills — sold as a subscription or included with training — would be a first-mover product.

## Strategic assessment: Is this a defensible niche?

**The case for yes:**

The demand signals are real. 63% of vibe coding users are non-developers. The vibe coding market is $4.7 billion and growing at 38% CAGR. Marketing content about Claude Code is proliferating. Anthropic is investing $100 million to grow the Claude ecosystem. The "citizen developer" movement is producing 100–120 million builders globally. Gartner predicts 80% of technology products will be built by non-technologists by 2026.

The competitive whitespace is genuine. Zero competitors occupy the specific intersection of "Claude Code training for marketing teams." The adjacent spaces (general vibe coding education, general AI marketing education) are either too generic or too expensive for the target buyer.

The business model fits Gregory's preferences. A training practice built around Claude Code for marketers is inherently a builder's business — you're building curricula, building skills libraries, building demonstration tools, building community infrastructure. Each cohort produces reusable assets. The work compounds.

The Anthropic ecosystem provides structural support. The Claude Partner Network, Anthropic Academy materials, and forthcoming certifications give a Claude-focused practice legitimacy, resources, and potential co-marketing support that a tool-agnostic practice wouldn't have.

**The case for caution:**

The persona is real but narrow. "Marketers who are ready to go from prompting to building" is a subset of a subset. The addressable market is tens of thousands, not millions. Revenue ceiling for a solo practice focused exclusively on this niche is probably $300K–$500K annually — substantial, but not venture-scale.

Platform risk is inherent. Building an entire practice around Claude Code means dependency on Anthropic's product decisions, pricing changes, and ecosystem strategy. If Anthropic launches its own "Claude Code for Marketing" training (they haven't, but could), the competitive advantage evaporates. Mitigation: teach principles and workflows that transfer across tools (Claude Code, Cursor, Lovable), with Claude as the primary but not exclusive platform.

The skill threshold is a real barrier. Despite the "no coding required" messaging, Claude Code still requires terminal comfort, file system understanding, and debugging capability. Many marketers will hit a wall at installation. Lovable and Bolt.new (browser-based, zero setup) are more accessible starting points. A training program needs a scaffolded on-ramp — not "open your terminal on day one."

Free alternatives will proliferate. Anthropic's own courses are free. CCforEveryone is free. YouTube tutorials are free. The premium value must come from something free resources can't provide: live expert instruction, marketing-specific use cases, implementation accountability, a curated skills library, and community. If the only differentiator is "organized information," it will be commoditized quickly.

**Net assessment:** This is a defensible niche for a solo practice if — and only if — the training is deeply specific to marketing use cases, includes hands-on building (not just conceptual education), and produces deliverables clients can't get from free resources. "Learn Claude Code" is not defensible. "Leave with five working Claude Code marketing tools your team will use Monday morning" is.

## Recommended positioning for Gregory

**Positioning statement:** Gregory helps marketing teams become builders — training tech-forward marketers to create custom tools, automations, and workflows with Claude Code instead of paying for off-the-shelf software that doesn't quite fit.

**Target persona (refined):** The 5–10% of marketing professionals who are already power users of Claude or ChatGPT, have experimented with automation tools (Zapier, Make.com), feel frustrated by the limitations of their current MarTech stack, and are ready to invest in the capability to build their own solutions. They're at companies doing $3M–$100M in revenue with marketing teams of 2–10 people. They're most likely to be found in e-commerce/DTC brands, digital agencies, and tech-forward startups.

**First product:** A 4-week live cohort — "Build Your Own MarTech" — at $1,500–$2,000/person, with 10–15 participants. Week 1: From prompting to building (Lovable for visual apps, then Claude Code fundamentals). Week 2: Build a custom reporting dashboard that replaces a paid tool. Week 3: Build a competitive intelligence system and content workflow. Week 4: Build a tool specific to each participant's team needs. Every participant leaves with 3–4 working tools and a Claude Code skills library for their team.

**Strategic advantage:** Join the Claude Partner Network on day one. It's free. Use the partner resources, Anthropic Academy materials, and eventually the certification to establish legitimacy. No competitor in the marketing training space has this structural relationship with Anthropic.

**Revenue model progression:** Cohort training ($150K–$300K year one) → productized skills library subscription ($50–$150/month) → corporate workshops for larger marketing teams ($5K–$15K/engagement) → eventually, a micro-SaaS tool born from patterns discovered in training delivery.

**What to avoid:** Don't position as "vibe coding for marketers" — the term is already being commoditized and carries negative connotations (security concerns, "AI slop" associations). Position as "custom tool building for marketing teams" — capability-focused, outcome-focused, professional.
